# ECONOMICS.md — Unit Economics

## How this product could make money

AIAudit itself is not really the business. The audit is more of a lead
generation tool for Credex.

The idea is:
- startups run a free audit
- discover overlapping AI subscriptions or unnecessary spend
- larger teams may want help consolidating infrastructure or reducing API costs
- Credex becomes the next step for companies with meaningful AI spend

So the economics mostly depend on whether the audit can generate qualified
high-intent leads consistently.

---

## Estimated operating cost

The product is relatively cheap to run.

Main costs:
- OpenAI API usage for summaries
- Vercel hosting
- Supabase database/storage
- transactional emails via Resend

At the current scale, most infrastructure fits comfortably within free tiers.

Even with moderate usage, a single audit is probably only a few cents at most,
especially if lightweight models are used for AI summaries.

The expensive part long-term is not infrastructure — it’s user acquisition.

---

## Rough conversion assumptions

These numbers are assumptions, not validated production metrics.

My current rough thinking:

- around 30–40% of visitors complete the audit
- maybe 15–25% leave their email if meaningful savings are shown
- a smaller percentage book a consultation or reply to outreach

The strongest leads are probably teams already spending heavily on AI tools,
especially engineering-heavy startups using multiple subscriptions across teams.

---

## Why the economics could work

The interesting part is that the product does not need a massive number of
customers to become valuable.

If one converted customer is worth a few hundred dollars in long-term value,
then even a relatively small number of high-quality leads could justify the
cost of running the product.

This works much better as:
- an organic acquisition channel
- a content/distribution tool
- a credibility builder

rather than a heavily paid-advertising business.

I don’t think paid acquisition would work well early on unless the average
customer value becomes much larger.

---

## Biggest economic risk

The biggest risk is probably that teams see AI spend as "worth it" even when
there is overlap.

Several people I interviewed said they knowingly keep multiple subscriptions
because different tools are better at different tasks.

That means the product cannot position itself as:
"cut all your AI costs."

It has to position itself more carefully around:
- visibility
- utilization tracking
- team-wide insights
- identifying obvious waste
- understanding overlap

rather than aggressive cost-cutting.

---

## Long-term opportunity

If AI tooling continues fragmenting across teams, there’s a good chance
companies eventually need:
- centralized visibility
- seat management
- usage analytics
- budgeting controls
- procurement workflows

The current version is only a lightweight audit tool, but the broader category
could evolve into a real internal operations platform for managing AI spend.
